Politics and Railroad Expansion

Railroads in the nineteenth century were inseparable from politics. State legislatures issued their corporate charters and determined where their lines would run. Localities, counties, cities, and towns, all bid for the favor of the companies and attempted to lure them through their territories. The federal government too played a role early on, as the Illinois Central in 1850 gained the first land grant to build its line--a whopping 2.9 million acres. The Union Pacific, the Burlington, and other Pacific railroads followed in the 1860s as land grants were provided to encourage the transcontinentals. Political parties--Whig, Republican, and Democrat--sought to turn these powerful entities to their electoral advantage. Railroads enabled new forms of campaigning. The Lincoln-Douglas debates in 1858 followed the new rail lines, and Stephen Douglas in 1860 conducted one the first modern campaign swings using railroads across the U.S. in his bid for the presidency. Railroad political interests grew throughout the nineteenth century, as lobbyists and politicians turned to the big corporations for patronage and in exchange railroads expected in most cases influence and favorable treatment. This App tracks party electoral results over time at the county level with the advancement of the railroad network.
